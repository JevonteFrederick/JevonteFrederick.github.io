Professional Self-assessment

My name is Jevonte Frederick. I am a computer science major from New Jersey. To start, my interest in computer science began when I grew up using modern technology as a child. Video game consoles and personal computers always fascinated me. I spent time on the internet from an early age that steered me towards learning more about the technology I use every day. After taking my introduction to programming languages in high school, I could not think of a greater interest in a career than computer science. This has inspired me to pursue a career as a software engineer or developer. Now, I am proud to earn a Bachelor of Science degree and move forward into my professional career.

After taking part in the computer science program at Southern New Hampshire University (SNHU), I have gained a varied skillset and knowledge base to carry into my professional career. Among these skills I have strengths of consistency, determination, innovation, organization, and detail-orientation. Consistency is my ability to follow best practices in coding and software development across multiple project goals and complexity. This is important to have so that stakeholders can expect the same professional standard and quality from my projects. Another skill I possess is determination, as finding the solution to a real-world problem is not always the easiest path to success. When technical difficulties or development setbacks occur, continuing to work towards a solution or compromise without sacrificing the value of software is an important skill to have. A professional prone to giving up in the face of adversity will not be a successful one. It is also good to understand limits and weaknesses to collaborate as needed and overcome obstacles in a team environment. In a developer team, someone who may not be efficient with configuring APIs can get assistance from a more knowledgeable teammate, and in return they can provide that teammate with expertise in another area such as vulnerability assessment. In the end, cooperation leads to better solutions and greater learning experience as professionals. However, determination is needed to address problems at their source.

Another skill I have is innovation, which is crucial for a computer scientist because developing software requires emerging solutions to deliver exceptional value. Most successful developers find solutions that satisfy an unfulfilled or highly desired goal for end-users. This solution often requires designing software that stands out, which requires a great deal of innovative planning, information gathering, and development to create emergent and competitive products. Also, one thing I have learned from computer science is how important organization and detail-oriented design are as a professional. Computer science requires a foundation of self-discipline for communication and collaboration. From my beginning as a computer science major, “spaghetti code” was a common phrase that emphasizes the need to structure and document work so that it is technically sound and readable for any audience. This is important to carry into a professional career as computer science is at the core of business systems and organization goals, so understanding the technology that powers enterprises must be well-communicated by its creators. This is why I have developed skills of organization and detail-orientation to maximize communication of knowledge and goals to align with others, whether it be fellow developers in a team, or business management that will rely on me to deliver valuable products that solve a given problem.

The courses I have taken in the computer science program helped me develop these skills overall. An incredibly significant course in my program path was Mobile Architecture and Programming. The project for this course taught me most about designing software with the user experience in mind by designing a mobile application. It was impactful in reinforcing development design and planning. The development of a mobile application is two equally important halves. First is the user interface of the app that has a guideline of constraints to optimize the user experience. Second, the application code needs to be technically sound and operate under the UI. Both the UI and code of the app need to be planned and designed efficiently, so it was one of the most extensive showcases of detail-oriented design and organization in my degree path. Other courses like Software Development Lifecycle were also impactful because it taught me about the real-world importance of collaboration and why communication and organizational skills matter as a professional computer scientist. The course highlights the development cycle of software and documents how a team works to align themselves with the goals of all stakeholders, including the organization they work for, their clients who receive deliverables, and the end-user that will use the product after deployment. The development of software has trended towards fast-paced development cycles that require extensive amounts of coordination, self-discipline, and morale to maintain a schedule for development lifecycles. From this course, I retained why I need to be organized and determined as a developer. My fellow team members will rely on me to be open to feedback and capable of aligning with collaborative efforts to improve and overcome weaknesses during development. This and similar courses also built upon the importance of security during and after development. Deploying software is not the end of development as vulnerability assessments, penetration testing, and action plans were key concepts I learned alongside the software development lifecycle. Now, developing for security is potentially the biggest element of software development as cyber security becomes continuously more critical in the modern day.

Another influential course was Computational Graphics and Visualization. This course was my formal introduction to computer graphics as the technology behind the digital media I have consumed throughout my life. Working with OpenGL to recreate an image in 3D was a memorable experience. It strengthened my creativity by creating complex objects with basic 3D shapes and programming dynamic lighting and textures gave me a new outlook on how computer graphics work. I learned how to approach problems by looking at the underlying systems and mechanics behind them. Something as simple as creating a cup in 3D made me think of how basic shapes can be scaled, positioned, and textured to create believable 3D objects. Other courses such as Software Testing, Automation, and Quality Assurance, further improved my skills as creating a test suite for software is the best way to demonstrate its capabilities. Before engaging with software testing automation, I ran code and did manual testing. Thanks to the software testing course, I learned to apply knowledge of test suites that can repeatedly test software for functionality, removing the tedium of manual testing. It also relates to the software development lifecycle as a development team should have a dedicated software tester that will make formal test cases that will be shared with others for quality assurance. So, I have knowledge of software testing and experience creating my test cases for quality assurance. In this ePortfolio I have chosen three artifacts to highlight the core concepts I learned from computer science. Those concepts are software design and engineering, Algorithms and data Structures, and databases. For software design and engineering, Artifact 1 is a compound interest calculator that I translated from C++ to Java. This artifact shows my consistency and organization as I was able to redevelop the software in a programming language that I am most familiar with and maintain its functionality. I made additional enhancements to the code by refactoring redundant code with modular methods and improved my documentation with more thorough comments to walk through the code. It highlights my understanding of professional writing and design by optimizing and documenting the structure and design flow of code. It also helps show my growth and awareness of different programming languages by accurately replicating C++ code in Java with improvements to present more professional work.

For algorithms and data structures, Artifact 2 is a mobile application project designing an app with a database to manage events. The enhancement implemented a queue for scheduling events to issue timed notifications though SMS messages. I also concentrated on security by eliminating the code stubs I had left in the artifact. There were design flaws that made the app more vulnerable and the code unorganized. This artifact highlights my ability to design effective computing solutions and motivated me to continue my experience developing mobile applications. The data flow of this application required me to apply knowledge of data structures and algorithms to design an effective way to issue priority-based notifications from a database. The time complexity was important as finding the most efficient algorithm to manage an extensive set of data is a requirement when developing valuable software. Otherwise, performance can be a significant weakness in the software as slow software will lead to a frustrating user experience.

For Databases, Artifact 3 is a project developing a contact service with multiple objects tested for deliverable requirements. I enhanced the project by implementing a database to store data for the individual object classes in separate tables. This highlights my understanding of databases as industry-standard technology that I initially learned from other courses in my degree path. I implemented the design patterns for a database and attached it to previous software with the potential of scalability in the future. It follows from previous work I have done creating a portable CRUD module in Python to power a front-facing website with a MongoDB database. With this artifact, I reapply my knowledge of databases by discovering more use cases with SQLite in Java and improved my capabilities of database deployment. Thanks to this artifact, I have experience with both remote and local databases so I have more depth on the ways they can be introduced and utilized. To continue improving, I will strive towards creating more complex software with databases using the fundamental knowledge I possess from enhancements such as this.
